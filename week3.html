Week 3: The Design Thinking Sprint

Date: October 8 - October 13, 2025

Quick Summary

  • Project Title: Smart Shoe Rack System for Mosque Entrances
  • Objective: To apply the Design Thinking process to design a human-centered solution that securely organizes shoes at mosque entrances, preventing theft and reducing anxiety for worshippers.
  • Outcome: A conceptual prototype for "The SecureShelf," a modular shelving unit with token-operated locking compartments.

Skills Learned:

  • Human-Centered Research: Conducting observational research and developing user empathy.
  • Problem Framing: Synthesizing user data into a clear, actionable Problem Statement using a specific formula.
  • Divergent & Convergent Thinking: Brainstorming a wide range of ideas (ideation) and then selecting the most promising one using defined criteria.
  • Rapid Prototyping: Communicating a complex idea through simple, annotated sketches.
  • Iterative Mindset: Understanding that design is a non-linear process of learning and refinement.

Digital Tools and Software Used:

  • Primary Tools: Pen and Paper (for empathy maps, brainstorming, and sketching).
  • Conceptual Tools: Empathy Map, Problem Statement Formula, Feasibility-Desirability-Viability Framework.

Topics:

  • Design Thinking Methodology
  • User Empathy and Research
  • Problem Definition
  • Creative Ideation and Brainstorming
  • Solution Selection and Prioritization
  • Low-Fidelity Prototyping (Sketching)

Introduction

This project involved participating in a condensed Design Thinking sprint to address a common issue in community spaces: the disorganized and insecure storage of personal belongings. The focus was on designing a "Smart Shoe Rack System" for mosques to prevent theft ("anti-stolling"). The goal was not to build a final product but to learn and apply the human-centered process of Design Thinking, moving from initial user empathy to a tangible prototype concept.

Process Documentation

The process followed the five stages of Design Thinking in a sprint format:

1. Empathize: The process began by observing and understanding the user (the worshipper). An Empathy Map was used to document what users Say, Do, Think, and Feel, revealing deep-seated anxiety and distraction caused by the current chaotic shoe situation.

2. Define: Insights from the empathy stage were synthesized into a clear Problem Statement: "A Worshipper needs a way to securely and efficiently store their shoes before prayer because the current free-for-all system causes anxiety about theft and loss, which distracts from their spiritual focus."

3. Ideate: A timed brainstorming session generated over 12 diverse solutions, ranging from simple numbered shelves to a high-tech robotic "shoe hotel." The rule was "quantity over quality" to encourage creative freedom.

4. Select: Ideas were evaluated based on Feasibility, Desirability, and Viability. A modular shelving unit with token-operated locks ("The SecureShelf") was selected as the most balanced and promising concept.

5. Prototype: The selected idea was translated into an annotated sketch, detailing the locking mechanism, token system, ventilation, and sloped shelves for easy retrieval. This sketch served as the final prototype for the sprint.

Reflection

This sprint was a powerful demonstration of how a structured creative process can lead to innovative and practical solutions. The most significant takeaway was the importance of the Empathize and Define stages. By truly understanding the user's emotional state (anxiety, distraction) rather than just the surface-level problem (stolen shoes), the solution became about restoring "peace of mind," not just securing footwear. This human-centered focus ensured the final concept was meaningful.

The creation of the Empathy Map, in particular, was transformative. Filling in the "Think" and "Feel" quadrants moved the project beyond a simple design brief into the realm of genuine user experience. Furthermore, seeing the list of 10+ ideas visually demonstrated the power of deferred judgment, as wild ideas sparked more practical ones. Finally, the annotated sketch forced a consideration of practical details that would have been overlooked in a vague description, proving that a prototype's value lies in communication, not artistic skill.

If I were to iterate, the next step would be to Test the sketch prototype with actual worshippers and mosque administrators. Their feedback on our visual concept would likely reveal new insights—perhaps about the cost of tokens or the speed of the system during peak times—that would send us back to the Ideate or Define stage, perfectly illustrating the iterative, non-linear nature of Design Thinking. This process proves that great design is not about having a single brilliant idea, but about following a disciplined path to discover the best solution for the people you are designing for.
