Week 3: The Design Thinking Sprint

Date: October 8 - October 13, 2025

Quick Summary

  • Skills Learned: Human-Centered Research, Problem Framing, Divergent & Convergent Thinking, Rapid Prototyping, Iterative Mindset
  • Digital Tools and Software Used: Pen and Paper, Empathy Map, Problem Statement Formula, Feasibility-Desirability-Viability Framework
  • Topics: Design Thinking Methodology, User Empathy and Research, Problem Definition, Creative Ideation and Brainstorming, Solution Selection and Prioritization, Low-Fidelity Prototyping
  • Key Concepts: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test, User-Centered Design
  • Challenges Faced: Balancing creativity with practicality, synthesizing user insights into actionable problem statements, selecting the most promising solution from multiple options

Introduction

This project involved participating in a condensed Design Thinking sprint to address a common issue in community spaces: the disorganized and insecure storage of personal belongings. The focus was on designing a "Secure Shoe Rack System" for mosques to prevent theft. The goal was not to build a final product but to learn and apply the human-centered process of Design Thinking, moving from initial user empathy to a tangible prototype concept.

Key Insight: The real problem wasn't just stolen shoes, but the anxiety and distraction this caused for worshippers, affecting their spiritual experience.

Process Documentation

The process followed the five stages of Design Thinking in a sprint format. This human-centered approach ensured our solution would address real user needs rather than just technical specifications.

1. Empathize: Understanding the User Experience

The process began by observing and understanding the user (the worshipper). An Empathy Map was used to document what users Say, Do, Think, and Feel, revealing deep-seated anxiety and distraction caused by the current chaotic shoe situation.

SAY

  • "I hope my shoes are still here after prayer"
  • "Where should I put my shoes?"
  • "I'll just wear cheaper shoes to the mosque"

DO

  • Hide shoes in corners
  • Place shoes far from entrance
  • Check on shoes during prayer
  • Rush out after prayer to retrieve shoes

THINK

  • "Someone might take my shoes by mistake"
  • "Are my shoes safe here?"
  • "I wish there was a better system"

FEEL

  • Anxious about shoe security
  • Distracted during prayer
  • Frustrated with the disorganization
  • Worried about losing expensive footwear

2. Define: Synthesizing the Core Problem

Insights from the empathy stage were synthesized into a clear Problem Statement using the formula: [User] needs to [user's need] because [insight].

Problem Statement: "A Worshipper needs a way to securely and efficiently store their shoes before prayer because the current free-for-all system causes anxiety about theft and loss, which distracts from their spiritual focus."

This problem statement shifted the focus from "preventing shoe theft" to "enabling spiritual focus through secure storage," which fundamentally changed our approach to the solution.

3. Ideate: Generating Creative Solutions

A timed brainstorming session generated over 12 diverse solutions, ranging from simple numbered shelves to high-tech automated systems. The rule was "quantity over quality" to encourage creative freedom without judgment.

  • Numbered shelves with matching tokens
  • Lock and key compartments for individual storage
  • QR code system with smartphone integration
  • Biometric lockers using fingerprint recognition
  • Color-coded system with matching wristbands
  • Automated shoe conveyor with retrieval tickets
  • Smart shelves with weight sensors
  • Community honor system with monitored area
  • Transparent lockers for visual verification
  • Modular rack system with expandable capacity
  • Mobile app integration for remote monitoring
  • Traditional lockers with combination locks

Solution Selection Criteria

Ideas were evaluated based on three key criteria:

Feasibility

Can we technically build this with available resources?

Desirability

Do users actually want and need this solution?

Viability

Is this sustainable and cost-effective long-term?

After evaluation, a modular shelving unit with lock and key compartments was selected as the most balanced and promising concept.

4. Prototype: Bringing the Solution to Life

The selected idea was translated into an annotated sketch, detailing the locking mechanism, key system, ventilation, and sloped shelves for easy retrieval. The lock and key approach was chosen for its familiarity, reliability, and cost-effectiveness.

Lock & Key System

Individual keys for each compartment with master key for administration

Modular Design

Stackable units that can expand as needed for different mosque sizes

Ventilation

Proper airflow to prevent odor buildup in enclosed spaces

Sloped Shelves

Angled design for easy shoe insertion and retrieval

Reflection

Learning Outcomes

This sprint was a powerful demonstration of how a structured creative process can lead to innovative and practical solutions. The most significant takeaway was the importance of the Empathize and Define stages. By truly understanding the user's emotional state (anxiety, distraction) rather than just the surface-level problem (stolen shoes), the solution became about restoring "peace of mind," not just securing footwear. This human-centered focus ensured the final concept was meaningful.

The creation of the Empathy Map, in particular, was transformative. Filling in the "Think" and "Feel" quadrants moved the project beyond a simple design brief into the realm of genuine user experience. Furthermore, seeing the list of 12+ ideas visually demonstrated the power of deferred judgment, as wild ideas sparked more practical ones. Finally, the annotated sketch forced a consideration of practical details that would have been overlooked in a vague description, proving that a prototype's value lies in communication, not artistic skill.

Iterative Mindset: Design Thinking is not a linear process but a cycle of learning and refinement. Each stage informs and improves the others.

Next Steps and Future Iterations

If I were to iterate, the next step would be to Test the sketch prototype with actual worshippers and mosque administrators. Their feedback on our visual concept would likely reveal new insights—perhaps about the practicality of key management or the system's efficiency during peak prayer times—that would send us back to the Ideate or Define stage, perfectly illustrating the iterative, non-linear nature of Design Thinking.

This process proves that great design is not about having a single brilliant idea, but about following a disciplined path to discover the best solution for the people you are designing for. The lock and key system emerged as the optimal balance between security, usability, and practicality for the mosque environment.
